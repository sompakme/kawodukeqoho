Blackberry Glycemic Index: Nutrition Facts, Weight Loss, Health Benefits

 Posted on August 9, 2024  |  4 minutes  |  800 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

Blackberries, known for their exquisite taste and vibrant color, are a delicious summer treat and a potential ally in managing blood sugar levels. With a low glycemic index and rich in essential nutrients like vitamins C and K, blackberries are emerging as a diabetic-friendly fruit option. 

​

Blackberries are also a notable source of dietary fiber, providing approximately 5.3 grams per 100 grams, which can aid in maintaining stable blood sugar levels and promoting digestive health.¹ This article aims to explore the nuanced relationship between blackberries and glycemic control, shedding light on their potential as a valuable addition to a balanced diet for individuals with diabetes.

Glycemic Index Table

Blackberries, with a serving size of 100 grams, possess a relatively low glycemic index of around 25, making them a favorable choice for individuals concerned about blood sugar management.² With approximately 9.61 grams of carbohydrates per 100-gram serving, blackberries can be incorporated into a balanced diet without causing significant spikes in blood glucose levels.¹ Considering the glycemic load (GL) per serving, which is calculated by multiplying the glycemic index by the grams of carbohydrate and then dividing by 100, the GL of blackberries per 100 grams comes to 2.53. The low GL signifies that blackberries have a minimal impact on blood sugar levels. It is worth noting that while cooking does soften the berry, it does not significantly affect the glycemic index, allowing individuals to enjoy the health benefits of blackberries, both raw and cooked.

​

The glycemic index (GI) is a relative ranking of carbohydrates in foods according to how they affect blood glucose levels. Foods with a low GI value (55 or less) are more slowly digested, absorbed, and metabolized, causing a lower and slower rise in blood glucose levels. Conversely, foods with a high GI value (70 or more) cause a rapid increase in blood glucose.

​

While some cooking methods can affect the glycemic index of certain foods, the impact on blackberries is minimal. Nonetheless, it's essential to note that combining blackberries with high-GI foods could affect the overall glycemic load of a meal, potentially causing a more significant impact on blood sugar levels.

Nutritional Facts

Blackberries are a nutrient-dense fruit, boasting a wealth of vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. A 100-gram serving of blackberries contains approximately 43 calories, 5.3 grams of dietary fiber, and 5.3 grams of sugar, making them a satisfying and relatively low-calorie snack option.¹ Additionally, blackberries are a rich source of vitamin C, with approximately 21 milligrams per 100 grams, contributing to their immune-boosting properties and overall health benefits.¹

Health Benefits

Is Blackberry Good for Weight Loss?

Blackberries can be a valuable component of a weight loss regimen due to their low-calorie content, high fiber levels, and significant water content, contributing to increased satiety and reduced overall calorie intake. Additionally, the presence of vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants in blackberries can support overall health while promoting weight management. Incorporating blackberries into a well-balanced diet and regular physical activity may aid in weight loss efforts. However, it's crucial to maintain a calorie-controlled diet and consider blackberries as part of an overall healthy eating plan to achieve sustainable weight loss goals.

Is Blackberry Safe for People Living with Diabetes?

Blackberries, with their relatively low glycemic index and rich fiber content, can be a safe and beneficial addition to the diets of individuals with diabetes. The fiber in blackberries can help regulate blood sugar levels and improve insulin sensitivity, potentially aiding in better glycemic control. However, it's crucial for individuals with diabetes to consume blackberries in moderation as part of a well-balanced diet, as excessive intake might still affect blood sugar levels. Consulting with a healthcare provider or a registered dietitian can help individuals with diabetes integrate blackberries safely into their meal plans, ensuring optimal management of their condition.

Allergies

Allergies to blackberries are relatively uncommon but can occur, especially in individuals with a history of berry allergies. Symptoms may include itching, swelling, hives, or, in severe cases, anaphylaxis. Those with known sensitivities to other berries or a predisposition to oral allergy syndrome might be at a higher risk for blackberry allergies. It is advisable for individuals with a history of food allergies to consult with an allergist before incorporating blackberries into their diet.
